# Layer 1: The Lifecycle Map

**AI Transformation Practice · OSI · UC San Diego**
Mapping stage artifacts. Draft templates, v1.

---

## What Layer 1 is for

Layer 1 produces the lifecycle map: a written, agreed account of how a service works from start to finish, including its stages, points of contact, handoffs, decisions, and the places where there's friction. The map exists so that Layer 2 (where AI opportunities get named) and Layer 3 (governance) rest on how the service really runs, not on how the org chart says it runs.

Layer 1 describes the service as it is and proposes no fixes; that is Layer 2's job. If a solution suggests itself while mapping, set it aside and keep mapping.

One person from the practice leads each engagement. The lead works alongside the lifecycle owner, who owns the service, and the business owners, who run it day to day. The stage ends when there is an agreed lifecycle map and an honest account of where the service has friction, signed by the lifecycle owner.

## How the set fits together

Use the templates in order. Each one feeds the next, and the last pulls the rest together into the packet that closes the stage.

1. **Interview guide** (`01`). How you gather the raw material. Use it before mapping anything, and come back to it whenever the map has gaps. Interviews are the source for everything else.

2. **Journey map** (`02`). The experience view: what the person the service is for does, stage by stage, and how it feels from their side. Built mostly from interviews and from watching the work happen.

3. **Service blueprint** (`03`). The operations view, lined up with the same stages as the journey map. What staff do in front of the person and behind the scenes, the systems behind both, and every handoff and decision. Handoffs and decisions are where work breaks, so this is the view Layer 2 starts from.

4. **Process documentation** (`04`). The step-by-step record underneath the blueprint. What sets off each step, who does it, on what system, under what rule, how long it takes, and what goes wrong. The fine detail that keeps the bigger views honest.

5. **Friction Register** (`05`). A running log of where the service has friction, each entry tied to a place on the map and to evidence. Built as you go, as the other four turn up problems.

6. **Validation packet** (`06`). The last step. Pulls the map together, records the review session, states the honest account of the friction, lists the decisions that carry into Layer 2, and captures the lifecycle owner's sign-off.

## How we use AI here

Two things are true at once, and holding both is the discipline of the layer.

We name no solutions here. Layer 1 describes the service as it is. If a fix suggests itself while mapping, set it aside and keep mapping.

We do use AI to help build the map. It lets us see the whole picture and hold more of what people tell us than any one reader could: tagging interview notes against a shared set of labels, finding where two accounts contradict each other, grouping related friction, and drafting first cuts of the views for a person to rebuild. AI helps us understand and capture. A person decides what is true.

And every use of AI is open to the people we talk to. We tell them how we use it, in plain terms, before they talk to us. Nothing a person says or does goes to AI in secret, AI never judges a person or scores the service, and a person checks everything AI produces before it counts. This is what protects the trust the interviews depend on.

## What "done" means

The Mapping stage is done when:

- The journey map and service blueprint cover the agreed scope from end to end and line up stage for stage.
- Every step in the process documentation traces to a stage on the map, and every handoff and decision in the blueprint is accounted for.
- The Friction Register rests on evidence, not impression, with severity and how often it happens written down.
- The lifecycle owner and the business owners have reviewed the map in a working session, and the changes from that session are in.
- The validation packet is signed.

## What carries into Layer 2

Layer 2 begins against this map. Two things travel forward and should be clean before sign-off:

- **The list of decisions.** Every point where the service makes a judgment, a routing choice, or an approval. These are the moments where AI might help, and Layer 2 will tag each by the value it could create.
- **The Friction Register.** The honest account of where the service has friction. Layer 2 weighs opportunity against friction, so a vague register produces vague opportunities.

Nothing in Layer 1 names an opportunity or proposes a fix; the layer describes the service as it is. Using AI to help build the map is a separate thing, allowed within the limits above.

## A note on scope

Set the scope before the first interview and write it on every artifact. A lifecycle can be mapped at different sizes (the whole employee lifecycle, or one piece of it such as performance appraisal). Pick the size with the lifecycle owner, write it down, and hold to it. Scope drift in Layer 1 is the most common way an engagement loses its footing.
