# Interview Guide

**Layer 1 · Lifecycle Map · Template 01**

Interviews are the raw material for the whole map. They show how the service actually runs, where the written process and the real one drift apart, and where the people who run it feel the friction. Do them before mapping, and come back to them whenever a view has gaps you cannot fill by watching the work.

Fill out one copy of this guide per interview. Keep the questions; adjust the wording to the person in front of you.

## How to run it

Open by saying plainly what this is and is not. It maps how the service works so the university can improve it. It is not a review of the person. Be just as plain about AI: we use it to help organize and cross-check what we hear, we will tell you how, it never judges you or scores your work, and nothing you say goes to it in secret. People speak more freely when they know you are documenting the service, not grading them, and when they know exactly what happens to their words.

Listen for the gap between the official process and the real one. The official version comes from documents. The real version comes from how people describe what they actually do, especially the workarounds. Workarounds matter: they mark where the service as designed fails the people inside it.

Follow the friction. When someone sighs, hedges, or says "well, usually," slow down and ask what happens the rest of the time. The exceptions are where the map gets interesting.

Get specifics, not summaries. "It takes a while" is not yet data. Ask: how long, how often, who is waiting, and what for.

## Questions

**The service in their words.**
- Walk me through what this service does, from your seat. Where does it start for you, and where does it end?
- Who is this service for, and what are they trying to get done?
- What does it look like when it goes well?

**Their part in it.**
- What is your role in this service, and where exactly do you pick it up and hand it off?
- Talk me through a recent real case, start to finish, the way it actually happened.
- What do you need from someone else before you can do your part, and what do they need from you?

**Points of contact and tools.**
- What systems or tools do you use at each step? Which ones help, and which get in the way?
- Where do people reach you or the service, and how?

**Handoffs.**
- Every time work leaves your hands or arrives in them, what moves and how? What gets lost or delayed in those moments?
- Who do you have to chase, and who chases you?

**Decisions.**
- Where in this service does someone have to make a judgment call, an approval, or a routing choice?
- What information do they decide on, and what happens when that information is missing or wrong?
- Which of those decisions are clear-cut, and which are genuinely hard?

**Where there's friction.**
- What is the most frustrating part of this service for you? For the people it serves?
- Where does work get redone? Where do errors show up, and how do you catch them?
- If you could fix one thing about this service tomorrow, what would it be, and what would change if you did?

**The unofficial version.**
- What do you do that is not written down anywhere?
- What would a new person get wrong because nobody tells them?

**Close.**
- Who else should I talk to, and what will they see that you cannot?
- What did I not ask that I should have?

## Raw capture

Write answers close to the person's own words. Don't polish them into tidy process language; the rough detail is where the friction shows. The tagging pass below works from what you actually wrote, so capture the words raw: a cleaned-up note gives the AI a cleaned-up service to tag.

## Tagging pass (after the interview)

Once the notes are down, mark them for what they feed later. AI does the first pass: it reads the notes, suggests a tag for each relevant passage, and shows the exact words that prompted each one. You confirm or reject every tag, and anything it is unsure of it flags for you rather than guessing. Nothing gets tagged without the words that justify it sitting right beside it. Keep your eye on the words, not the label.

| Tag | What to mark |
|---|---|
| STAGE | Anything that sets or bounds a stage of the journey |
| TOUCH | A point of contact or channel |
| HAND | A handoff between roles, units, or systems |
| DEC | A decision, approval, or routing point |
| FRICTION | A friction point (log it in the Friction Register, template 05) |
| WORKAROUND | An unofficial practice that diverges from the written process |
| CONFLICT | A point where this account contradicts another interview (flag for the review) |
